2 Nature loves stressors up to a point.

The strength of natural beings and systems is that they grow and heal when exposed to a certain level of stressors. These stresses and shocks should be accute with ample time for recovery afterwards.

3 "Someone who procrastinates is not irrational; it is his environment that is irrational"

Festina lente
'Make haste slowly'; latin expression
wu-wei
'passive achievement'; Doctrine coined by Lao Tzu

Procrastination is natural, it is the body using its natural defense against unknown environmnents. Letting things take care of themselves and letting them exercise their antifragility. Procrastination is a message from our natural willpower which we feel through low motivation. To change this response of the body, the environment needs to be changed to one where the body is not fighting its natural impulses. Pocrastination can be a very powerful tool for filtering information as it works as a naturalistic-risk-based form of decision making.

6 Randomness in my own life

My day has many random events in it. Most of these are within me, such as impulses to eat, to stand up, move, think of some random idea or perform some activity. Fewer random events are those outside of me such as people talking to me, household chores that come up, etc. To minimise the harm from random events and to make me less fragile I would first need focus on the randomness from internal impulses. I have a very strong habit to procrastinate from tasks for my research, this impulse usually pops up whenever I feel insecure about what I'm doing or I encounter difficulties, This happens randomly throughout the day and I cannot control when it happens or the fact that these emotions come up. What I can change is how I respond to these random events, in this response lies the current fragility. If I respond by looking at my phone, checking social media or mindlessly browsing the web, I incur large downsides as these activities destroy the focus I have built up and they provide habit-building dopamine hits which will make it more difficult to resist them in the future.

Now to devise a plan to limit my downsides of these random events, The most obvious way is to make it significantly more difficult for me to access the internet when I am working, to achieve this I will delete the wifi connection from my laptop, turn off my phone and hide it in a drawer outside my room so that it is out of sight. Doing this changes my environment in such a way that I am less harmed by my own random impulses. In the framework of Taleb, this will reduce my fragility to random events, it will make me more robust.

The ultimate goal is of course to make my behaviour antifragile, this would mean that I would have more upsides than downsides from random events. I am not sure how I could apply this to my procrastination behaviour, perhaps it would be something along the lines of seeing the impulses as a signal to switch up what I am working on in such a way to keep my motivation high and in turn my productivity high. Although I currently think that there are too many impulses and they occur too frequently, so that using them as signals would be very difficult.

I do believe that focusing on decreasing my fragility first is a fantastic way to start, this is exactly like the 'Via Negative' concept Taleb explained, where it is often better to look for things to subtract from your life in order to improve it.

The main point here is that I should use my environment to control my reaction to random events, especially internal random impulses.

In other words, if something is fragile, its risk of breaking makes anything you do to improve it or make it "efficient" inconsequential unless you first reduce that risk of breaking. As Publilius Syrus wrote, nothing can be done both hastily and safely-almost nothing.

7 The barbell strategy

Put the majority of your effort/funds into a low risk option and a small percentage (10%) into high risk options. This can also be applied to research I think, the low risk option is producting results and writing papers about them, the high risk option is then going deeper into the analysis and trying to optimize or innovate small parts of it in the hope that this will produce novel insights or results. Currently I put way too much effort into high risk options as I am being a perfectionist in my behaviour, I am focusing too much on the minute details of the analysis, which has a lot more downsides than upsides. The best, most antifragile thing I could be doing right now is writing papers and drafts, this has basically only upsides as it has a high chance of resulting in publications and it will definitely give me the required experience in scientific writing.

For my personal behaviour and procrastination I can also apply this strategy, there are certain tasks which are very low risk for impulsive distractions. For example reading papers, especially when they are printed allows me to remove 90% of distractions from my desk and immediate vicinity. This should make it low risk as there are fewer impulses that I can give into easily. High risk tasks on the other hand are all the things I do which require and internet connection, these tasks allow for a very large amount of procrastination and they should be kept to a minimum in terms of time spent.

For antifragility is the combination aggressiveness plus paranoia-clip your downside, protect yourself from extreme harm, and let the upside, the positive Black Swans, take care of itself. We saw Seneca’s asymmetry: more upside than downside can come simply from the reduction of extreme downside (emotional harm) rather than improving things in the middle.

One finds similar ideas in ancestral lore: it is explained in a Yiddish proverb that says 'Provide for the worst; the best can take care of itself.'

8 The philosopher's stone

Take a model and see how it responds to small changes in the initial assumptions. This gives a test of the fragility of the model.

10 Medicine

Jensen's inequality in medicine_ People who suffer from accute respiratory distress syndrome used to be put on ventilators which provided constant pressure and volume. Research has shown that variations in pressure actually allows for more volume to be delivered to the lungs than constant pressure for the same mean value of pressure. Also spikes in lung pressure help open up collapsed alveoli (air cells in the lungs). From this we can see that lungs are antifragile to variations in pressure.

But the thought experiment can show how frequent visits to the doctor, particularly outside the cases of a life-threatening ailment or an uncomfortable condition—just like frequent access to information—can be harmful.

Good point here about how we often mistake the natural variability in things for information. The best way to reduce the harm from this is then of course to limit the frequent access to information, or at least limit the interpretation of such anecdotal information.

11 Big data

The number of correlations is convex with the number of variables for big datasets. This means that experiments which include a large number of variables will almost alway be able to find a correlation between some of them. These correlations are more often than not just spurious.
